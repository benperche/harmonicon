Identify from Bass Note

Select a bass note to see every chord in the chosen key that could have that note in the bass, along with the Roman numeral and inversion it implies.

How to read this: Click a chord to expand its inversions. The bass note (underlined in red) tells you which note is at the bottom of the chord — this determines the Roman numeral suffix. The chord tones column shows the notes of the chord in root-position order; the 7th is shown in italics (7th: X) and is optional — include it for a richer sound, omit it for a plain triad. In the 3rd inversion, the 7th is in the bass and is underlined like any other bass note.

Figured bass suffixes: No suffix = root position · ⁶ = 1st inversion (3rd in bass) · ⁶₄ = 2nd inversion (5th in bass) · ⁷ = root position 7th chord · ⁶₅ = 1st inv. 7th · ⁴₃ = 2nd inv. 7th · ⁴₂ = 3rd inv. 7th (7th in bass) · °⁷ °⁶₅ °⁴₃ °⁴₂ = diminished 7th and its inversions.

Special chords: Aug. 6th chords (It⁺⁶, Fr⁴₃, Ger⁶₅) have b̂6 in the bass and ♯^4 as the top note, creating an augmented 6th interval that resolves outward to the dominant. The Neapolitan (N⁶) is a major triad on ♭^2, almost always in 1st inversion, functioning as a pre-dominant. Borrowed chords are taken from the parallel minor (in a major key) or parallel major (in a minor key). CT chords share a common tone with the target harmony — the sustained note is shown in the chord name.
